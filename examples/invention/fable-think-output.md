Before picking a design: "losing the remote" hides at least four different
problems — can't find it NOW (retrieval), it migrates over time (drift),
it leaves the room (displacement), and needing it at all (dependence).
One option per mechanism, then kill-tests:

**1. Retrieval — TV-anchored finder.** The TV itself pings the remote
(press the TV's power stud, remote beeps). Kill-test: beats the phone-app
tracker because the failure moment is "remote lost AND phone not in hand ->
walk to the TV" — the TV is never lost. Survives.

**2. Drift — homing habit by design.** Charging cradle with magnetic
snap + the remote chirps softly if motionless outside the cradle for 4
hours. Kill-test: chirping is exactly the kind of nagging users disable on
day two; magnet-only cradle survives as a passive, zero-annoyance floor.

**3. Displacement — couch-proofing.** The top failure site is inside/under
furniture. A wide, flat, high-friction shape that cannot slip between
cushions attacks the #1 loss site with zero electronics. Kill-test:
form-factor change conflicts with TV-maker BOM conservatism — viable as an
aftermarket silicone sleeve instead. Survives, cheap.

**4. Dependence — remove the object.** Phone-as-remote / voice control.
Kill-test: fails the core user (the remote-loser skews toward NOT wanting
a phone step or a mic in the room). Killed for this problem, noted for
another market.

**Pick:** ship 1 + 3 together — TV-anchored ping (electronics, licensed to
TV makers) plus the anti-sink sleeve (aftermarket, sellable next quarter,
funds validation of 1). Cheapest falsification first: 50-unit sleeve pilot;
if under-couch losses aren't actually the top site in diary studies, kill 3
before tooling.
